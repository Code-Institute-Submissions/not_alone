Employment rates and what the row charts tell us

Take a look at the two row charts above.
You will notice that (as might be expected) the employment rate of people with mental illnesses is significantly lower than that of the general population.

We can see how the employment rates of the general population has fluctuated with the ebb and flow of the economy at the time, reaching it's lowest valley in 2010 with 69.42% of the nation in gainful employment, and it's highest peak in 2011, with a whopping 83.82% of our population secure in a job.

It then levelled out in 2014 to sit at around 71%, which would be considered relatively healthy.

Now take a look to your left at the employment rate of people suffering from a mental illness - This chart follows no such pattern and seems to be completely unnaffected by the economy.

The common theme here is that the numbers are drastically reduced.

The good news however, is that we can clearly see that things are steadily improving.

This would suggest that as attitudes change toward mental health and conversations open up, either more people with mental illnesses are being given a chance, or perhaps people are becoming more comfortable talking to their current employers about their conditions.

Either way you cut it, a leap from 26.92% in 2006 to 34.05% in 2014 is a fantastic step forward both for the welfare of the nations mental health, and the ongoing battle in removing the stigma attached to mental illness.

The numbers are still very far off from what they need to be, but if we continue to talk, support and be open, we can and will get them there.
